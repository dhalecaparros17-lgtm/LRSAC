üîé Research: IMRAD Format Explained

Step-by-Step Guide: Writing a Research Paper (IMRAD Format)

  1. Introduction
    • Purpose: Introduce your topic, explain its importance, and state your research question or hypothesis.
    • How to write:
      • Start broad: What is your topic about?
      • Why is it important or interesting? (Relate to real life or current issues.)
      • What is already known? (Brief background, cite sources.)
      • End with: ‚ÄúThis study aims to...‚Äù
    • Example:
      ‚ÄúMobile phones are used daily by students for both learning and entertainment. However, the effect of mobile phone use on academic performance is debated. This study aims to determine if increased mobile phone usage negatively affects high school students‚Äô grades.‚Äù
  2. Method (Methodology)
    • Purpose: Explain exactly how you did your study so others can repeat it.
    • How to write:
      • Describe your participants or materials (e.g., ‚Äú50 Grade 11 students from Gumaca NHS‚Äù).
      • Explain your procedure: surveys, experiments, observations?
      • Include your tools: questionnaires, measuring instruments, or software.
      • Be clear and step-by-step.
    • Example:
      ‚ÄúThis study surveyed 50 Grade 11 students from Gumaca NHS. A questionnaire asked about daily mobile phone use and average grades. Data were collected in July 2025 and analyzed for patterns.‚Äù
  3. Results
    • Purpose: Present what you found, without opinion.
    • How to write:
      • Summarize your data clearly ‚Äî use tables or charts if possible.
      • Mention patterns, averages, and key findings.
      • Don‚Äôt explain or interpret yet ‚Äî just state facts.
    • Example:
      ‚ÄúThe average phone usage among students was 5 hours/day. Students using phones for more than 6 hours/day had an average grade of 82, while those under 4 hours/day averaged 88. See Table 1 for details.‚Äù
  4. Discussion
    • Purpose: Explain what your results mean, connect to previous studies, suggest improvements or next steps.
    • How to write:
      • What do your findings suggest? (e.g., ‚ÄúMore phone use may be linked to lower grades.‚Äù)
      • Are your results similar or different from other studies?
      • Any limitations (things you couldn‚Äôt control)?
      • What should future research do?
    • Example:
      ‚ÄúThese results support the idea that heavy phone usage is associated with lower grades. This matches previous studies in other schools. However, we only surveyed one grade level. Future studies should include more schools and consider different types of phone use.‚Äù
  5. References
    • Purpose: List all sources you used (books, websites, journals).
    • How to write:
      • Follow your teacher‚Äôs required format (APA, MLA, etc.).
      • Include author, title, source, and year for each reference.
    • Example (APA):
      ‚ÄúSmith, J. (2020). The effects of technology on learning. Educational Journal, 15(2), 55-60.‚Äù

üìä Statistics: Modules, Examples & Advanced Topics

1. What is Statistics?

  • Statistics is the science of collecting, organizing, analyzing, and interpreting data. It helps us understand large amounts of information by summarizing and finding patterns.
  • Example: In a class survey, you record each student‚Äôs favorite subject and find out which is most popular.

2. Types of Data

  • Qualitative (Categorical): Data that describes qualities (e.g., colors, brands, names). Example: ‚ÄúBlue‚Äù, ‚ÄúRed‚Äù, ‚ÄúGreen‚Äù (favorite color survey).
  • Quantitative (Numerical): Data that can be measured or counted (e.g., heights, scores, ages). Example: ‚Äú150 cm‚Äù, ‚Äú162 cm‚Äù, ‚Äú170 cm‚Äù (students‚Äô heights).

3. Collecting & Organizing Data

  1. Decide on your data source: survey, experiment, or observation.
  2. Use a table or spreadsheet (Google Sheets, Excel) to record responses.
  3. Check for errors and organize data into categories or order.
  4. Example:
    Survey: ‚ÄúHow many hours do you study per day?‚Äù
    Table:
    NameHours
    Anna2
    Ben1
    Chloe3
    David2

4. Descriptive Statistics (with Examples)

  • Mean (Average): Add up all numbers, then divide by how many. Example: (2+1+3+2) √∑ 4 = 2.
  • Median: Middle value when arranged in order. Example: For 1,2,2,3, median is (2+2)/2 = 2.
  • Mode: Value that appears most often. Example: In 2,1,3,2: mode is 2 (it appears twice).
  • Range: Highest minus lowest. Example: 3 - 1 = 2.

5. Visualizing Data

  • Turn numbers into pictures to spot trends ‚Äî use bar graphs, pie charts, or line graphs.
  • How to do it: Use Google Sheets or Meta-Chart to enter your data and choose a chart style.
  • Example: Plot students‚Äô study hours as a bar graph to see which is most common.

6. Probability Basics

  • Probability = likelihood of something happening.
  • Formula: \\( P(\text{event}) = \frac{\text{Number of favorable outcomes}}{\text{Total possible outcomes}} \\)
  • Example: What‚Äôs the chance of rolling a 4 on a die? There is 1 ‚Äú4‚Äù and 6 sides: \\( P = \frac{1}{6} \\)

7. Hypothesis Testing (Step-by-Step)

  • Hypothesis testing checks if your results are statistically significant.
  • Step 1: State the null hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ: no effect) and alternative hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ: there is an effect).
  • Step 2: Choose a significance level (usually 0.05).
  • Step 3: Collect data and calculate a test statistic (e.g., t-test or z-test).
  • Step 4: Find the p-value. If p-value < significance level, reject H‚ÇÄ.
  • Example: You want to test if a new study method improves test scores. H‚ÇÄ: ‚ÄúNo improvement.‚Äù H‚ÇÅ: ‚ÄúThere is improvement.‚Äù After analysis, p-value = 0.03, so you reject H‚ÇÄ.
  • Hypothesis Testing: Khan Academy Video

8. Regression (Explained & Example)

  • Regression shows the relationship between two variables (e.g., study time and grades).
  • Simple linear regression formula: \\( y = mx + b \\), where y = predicted value, m = slope, x = variable, b = intercept.
  • How to do it: Plot your data on a graph, draw the best-fit line, and use the equation to make predictions.
  • Example: If your regression line is Grades = 2.5 √ó (Study hours) + 80, then studying 4 hours predicts a grade of 90.
